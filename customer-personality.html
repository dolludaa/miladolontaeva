Customer Personality Analysis

Behavioral and demographic analysis to identify value-driving segments and inform marketing strategies

TL;DR

In this project, I conducted a behavioral and demographic analysis based on customer data from a retail business. The goal was to identify value-driving segments, understand product/channel performance, and inform marketing and engagement strategies. The final Tableau story included three dashboards:

  • Segment Overview
  • Purchase & Channel Behavior
  • High-Value Segment Deep Dive
Check out the project on Tableau Public

Key Insights and Recommendations

Customer Value and Segments

  • Customers with no children consistently spend more than other groups, averaging over $1,000 per person.
  • Households without kids are the most responsive to campaigns and more likely to buy premium categories (wine, meat, gold).

Product Strategy

  • Wine is the highest-value product: $680,816 total revenue and $304 per customer — more than 11× the spend on sweets or fruits.
  • Gold has lower volume but shows strong value per buyer, making it ideal for targeted promotions.

Channel Performance

  • Web channel is the most stable over time, maintaining average purchases across recency groups.
  • Store dominates in volume but decays faster in retention, while Catalog shows sharp drop-off.

Analytical Process

1. Understanding the Business Task

The main business objectives were:

  • Identify high-spending and high-response customer segments
  • Understand how demographic traits influence behavior
  • Connect product/channel trends to segment structure

Guiding questions:

  • Who are our highest-value customers?
  • How does family size impact product mix?
  • Which channels deliver consistent engagement?
  • What product categories yield highest revenue per user?

2. Data Preparation and Cleaning

The dataset included demographics, campaign response, product spend, and transactional info.

I performed all data cleaning and structuring in Excel before loading into Tableau.

Key cleaning steps:

  • Merged child/teen counts into a single "Family Type" column
  • Created three family segments: Full Family, Small Family, No Kids
  • Grouped birth years into age bins
  • Unified education and marital status categories
  • Removed customers with $0 spend
  • Aggregated 6 campaign response flags into a simplified "Response" field

3. Data Analysis and Visualization

Once cleaned, I imported the dataset into Tableau and built a story containing three dashboards:

1. Segment Overview

This dashboard introduced core distributions:

  • Marital status and age (most customers are 40–65; high response in 25–35 and 70+)
  • Education vs. Income (PhD and Master's have higher income and stronger campaign acceptance)
  • Family Type split: Small Families (50%) dominate volume; No Kids (28%) dominate spend.
2. Purchase & Channel Behavior

Here I focused on how segments behave:

Spending by Product:

  • Wine leads with $680,816 in total sales
  • Meat is second with $373,968 and $167 per user
  • Gold shows premium potential ($44/user on average)

Channel Use & Recency:

  • Web shows stable purchase patterns across all recency buckets
  • Store has 42% more transactions than Web, but is more sensitive to recency
  • Catalog fades quickly, suggesting low retention

Frequency:

  • 65% of users made 10 or fewer purchases
  • Frequent buyers (>20 purchases) are under 7% of base
3. High-Value Segment Deep Dive

This dashboard isolates "No Kids" as the most valuable segment:

  • Avg. spend: $1,097
  • Highest response rate
  • Strongest use of Web + Store
  • Preference for wine ($487 avg.) and meat ($373)

Full Families spent the least (~$500) and leaned toward fish, sweets, and offline channels.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates that customer value is not spread evenly — it's concentrated in specific structural segments. Notably, households without children deliver higher revenue, stronger marketing response, and more consistent behavior over time.

Recommendations:

  • Target the No Kids segment with premium offers and retention campaigns
  • Use the Web channel as a stable re-engagement vector
  • Reduce reliance on Catalog, especially for dormant users
  • Promote high-margin products like wine and gold to top-performing segments

Strategic Focus: Instead of optimizing for total volume, strategy should prioritize value-per-customer and behavioral stability, both of which are strongest in the No Kids group.
